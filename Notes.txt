----------------------------------------------- Insights summary stats -------------------------------------------

Categorical Variables ------------------
- Target (ten_year_chd): 84.8% No vs 15.2% Yes — clear class imbalance that drives all downstream decisions 
around validation and modeling.

- Rare categories to watch: prevalent_stroke (only 25 Yes, 0.6%) is near-zero variance and may be unstable in the model. 
diabetes (109 Yes, 2.6%) is rare but clinically important — keep it but monitor its posterior carefully.

- bp_meds has 53 missing and only 3% exposed — rare and incomplete, missingness worth investigating. 
current_smoker is well balanced (50/50) and a strong candidate predictor. education is ordinal with 4 levels and 105 missing — decide early whether to treat as ordinal or dummy-coded.


Continuous Variables --------------------
- age is the cleanest variable — skewness of 0.2, no missing, well behaved. 
dia_bp and heart_rate are also relatively clean with mild skew and negligible missingness.

- glucose is the most problematic — skewness of 6.2, 388 missing, and a max of 394 which is only plausible in diabetic patients. If any of those extreme values are paired with diabetes = No, that's a data quality flag.
Missingness here is almost certainly not random (MNAR).

- tot_chol max of 696 is clinically implausible — typical clinical ceiling is around 400. 
Worth cross-checking and likely removing that observation. sys_bp max of 295 is extreme but possible in a hypertensive crisis — check if paired with bp_meds = Yes or prevalent_hyp = Yes. 
bmi of 56.8 is severe obesity but biologically plausible — keep it.

- cigs_per_day has median 0, mean 9, and skewness 1.2 — the zero inflation is entirely explained by non-smokers.
The real smoker distribution runs from roughly 1 to 30 (P95), which is reasonable.


((Points to Carry Forward))
- Class imbalance must be addressed through stratified splitting and validation metrics focused on AUC and calibration rather than accuracy.
Glucose needs the most careful imputation treatment given both its missingness volume and likely MNAR nature. 
The redundancy between cigs_per_day and current_smoker needs a decision before modeling. 
Scaling is essential given the wide range differences across variables.
And the extreme values in tot_chol and glucose should be investigated against other clinical variables before imputation.

------------------------------------------ Bivariate investigation -------------------------------------

Continuous Predictors by CHD Status -------------------------

- Age is the clearest separator — the CHD=Yes distribution is visibly shifted to the right, peaking around 55-60 compared
to 40-45 for CHD=No. This is the strongest visual signal among all continuous predictors and aligns well with the literature.

- Systolic BP shows a meaningful rightward shift for CHD=Yes, with a heavier tail at higher values.
The separation is not as clean as age but is clearly present. This is an expected and important predictor.

- Cigarettes per day shows a subtle but visible difference — the CHD=Yes group has a slightly heavier distribution in the 10-30 range 
relative to CHD=No. The zero spike is similar between groups, suggesting the smoking effect comes from quantity smoked
rather than smoking status alone. This is an argument for keeping cigs_per_day over current_smoker.

- Total cholesterol, diastolic BP, BMI, heart rate, and glucose show very little separation between CHD groups. 
The distributions nearly overlap completely. This doesn't mean they're unimportant — it means their effect may be modest
or mediated through other variables — but expect weaker posterior inclusion probabilities for these in SSVS.


Categorical Predictors by CHD Proportion -----------------------

- Prevalent stroke stands out here despite its rarity — roughly 40% of stroke patients developed CHD versus about 15% in the non-stroke group. 
This is a strong proportional signal, but the very small sample (25 cases) means this estimate is highly uncertain. 
The wide credible interval in your Bayesian model will reflect that uncertainty honestly.

- Diabetes tells a similar story — about 35% CHD rate among diabetics versus 15% in non-diabetics. 
Clinically meaningful and consistent with literature, though again the small n limits precision.

- Hypertension shows a clear difference — roughly 22% CHD rate among hypertensives versus about 12% in normotensives. 
Well-powered enough to be reliable given 31% prevalence.

- BP medication shows elevated CHD in the Yes group, but this is likely confounded — people on BP meds already have cardiovascular risk factors.
The association reflects underlying disease severity more than the medication itself. <<<<<<<<<<<<<<<<

- Current smoker shows a modest but visible difference, consistent with the continuous density plot observation.

- Sex shows males have noticeably higher CHD proportion than females — this is a well-established finding and should be a reliable predictor.

- Education shows a gradient — lower education levels associate with slightly higher CHD proportion, 
consistent with socioeconomic health literature, though the differences are modest.


Correlation Matrix ---------------------------------

- The most important finding here is the sys_bp / dia_bp correlation of 0.78 — this is substantial multicollinearity. Including both in the model simultaneously
will make their individual coefficients unstable and harder to interpret.
You should either drop one (dia_bp is typically the weaker CHD predictor), use only sys_bp, or be aware that
SSVS may select one and exclude the other depending on which explains more variance.

- cigs_per_day and current_smoker correlate at 0.77 — this confirms the redundancy we flagged earlier.
Keeping both is problematic. Given that the bivariate plot suggested cigs_per_day carries more signal, dropping current_smoker and keeping cigs_per_day is the more defensible choice.

- glucose and diabetes correlate at 0.62 — expected, but not so
high as to be a problem since they capture different things (continuous level vs clinical diagnosis). Keep both but monitor their posteriors together.

- sys_bp and prevalent_hyp correlate at 0.39 and age and sys_bp at 0.39 — moderate but not alarming. 
Worth watching in the posterior but unlikely to cause serious instability.

- Correlations with the outcome (ten_year_chd) are all modest, with age (0.23) and sys_bp (0.22) 
being the strongest, followed by tot_chol (0.15) and cigs_per_day (0.18).  This is consistent with the density plots.



((Key Decisions to Make Before Modeling))
The correlation matrix crystallizes two decisions you need to make explicitly: 
drop current_smoker in favor of cigs_per_day, and decide how to handle the sys_bp / dia_bp pair — either drop dia_bp or let SSVS handle it and report whichever gets selected. 
Both decisions should be documented and justified in your write-up.

>> Drop both sys_bp and dia_bp, replace with pulse_pressure = sys_bp - dia_bp, and cite the cardiovascular literature supporting it as an independent CHD predictor. Clean, justified, and interpretable.

Missingness Pattern Across All Rows ------------------------------------
The row-level pattern plot confirms that missingness is scattered randomly across the dataset with no obvious

systematic data collection failure at a specific time point, though it doesn't rule out MNAR.


Co-missingness (UpSet Plot) --------------------------------------
The dominant finding is that 331 observations have glucose missing alone, by far the largest bar and confirming that glucose missingness
is largely its own phenomenon. Similarly, 93 observations have education missing alone and 51 have bp_meds missing alone — these are 
all single-variable missingness, not co-occurrence.

- When it comes to actual co-missingness, the intersections are very small. The only one worth noting is tot_chol and glucose 
co-missing in 38 rows. Everything else is in single digits and essentially negligible. This is a clean and reassuring picture overall
